[

\mu':\mathbb R^8_v\otimes \mathbb R^8_- \to \mathbb R^8_+,

[

Let (b\in S^8) and let (v\in (E_v)*b) satisfy (|v|=1). The map
[
\gamma(v): (E*+)*b \to (E*-)*b,\qquad \gamma(v)(s)=\widetilde{\mu}(v\otimes s)
]
is an isomorphism of Euclidean vector spaces, and restricts to a diffeomorphism of unit spheres (S((E*+)*b)\to S((E*-)_b)). (In standard Clifford conventions, (\gamma(v)) is orthogonal and satisfies (\gamma(v)^2=\pm \mathrm{Id}).) ([CIS UPenn][1])

*Proof sketch.* This is the usual Clifford relation: for unit (v), the induced endomorphism on spinors is invertible with inverse proportional to itself. Standard references treat this in the construction of spin representations from Clifford algebras. ([CIS UPenn][1])

A smooth bundle map (\Phi:S(E_+)\to S(E_-)) of the form
[
\Phi_b(u_+) = \gamma(v(b))(u_+)
]

We give a rigorous geometric formulation of a triality-based construction involving chiral spinor sphere bundles over \(S^8\).
Using standard tools from Lie group theory, homogeneous spaces, and vector bundle topology,
we show that no global triality identification exists, but that a patchwise Clifford-multiplication construction
glues consistently to define a smooth \(15\)-dimensional manifold.
For the canonical principal bundle
\[
\mathrm{Spin}(9)\to \mathrm{Spin}(9)/\mathrm{Spin}(8)\cong S^8,
\]
the resulting space is identified with the associated half-spin sphere bundle,
whose total space is \( \mathrm{Spin}(9)/\mathrm{Spin}(7)\cong S^{15} \).
The induced fibration coincides with the classical octonionic Hopf fibration
\( S^7\hookrightarrow S^{15}\to S^8 \).
This clarifies the precise role of triality and chiral stabilizers in the geometry underlying the Hopf fibration.
\end{abstract}

## 4. Associated Bundles and Natural Dirac-Type Operators on the Hopf Background

### 4.1 Spin structures and homogeneous spinor bundles on (S^{15})

We fix the standard round sphere (S^{15}) with its canonical spin structure. Since (S^{15}) is simply connected and (H^1(S^{15};\mathbb{Z}_2)=0), the spin structure is unique up to isomorphism. Let
[
\mathrm{Spin}(15)\longrightarrow \mathrm{SO}(15)
]
be the double cover, and let (\mathbb{S}\to S^{15}) denote the associated real spinor bundle.

In our setting we use the homogeneous realization
[
S^{15}\cong G/H=\mathrm{Spin}(9)/\mathrm{Spin}(7),
]
so any natural vector bundle we will use is most efficiently described as a homogeneous associated bundle.

#### Homogeneous associated bundles

Let (V) be a finite-dimensional real representation of (H=\mathrm{Spin}(7)). Define the associated homogeneous vector bundle
[
E_V ;:=; G\times_H V ;\longrightarrow; G/H\cong S^{15}.
]
Sections of (E_V) can be identified with (H)-equivariant functions (f:G\to V) satisfying
[
f(gh)=h^{-1}f(g),\qquad g\in G,; h\in H.
]

This will be our universal template: *every* tensor/spinor bundle appearing below is of this form for an appropriate (H)-module (V).

### 4.2 Bundles induced from the Hopf fibration and the canonical connection

Recall from Sections 1–3 the homogeneous fibration
[

(G,K,H)=(\mathrm{Spin}(9),\mathrm{Spin}(8),\mathrm{Spin}(7)),

with canonical (G)-invariant connection defined by the reductive splitting
[
\mathfrak{g}=\mathfrak{k}\oplus\mathfrak{p},

We retain the induced orthogonal splitting

#### 4.2.1 Canonical induced connections on (E_V)

Let (E_V=G\times_H V). The canonical homogeneous connection on (G\to G/H) (defined by the (\mathfrak{h})-projection of the Maurer–Cartan form) induces a canonical covariant derivative
[
\nabla^{V}:\Gamma(E_V)\to \Gamma(T^\ast S^{15}\otimes E_V).
]

Concretely, if (s) corresponds to an (H)-equivariant function (f:G\to V), then (\nabla^V) differentiates (f) along the horizontal lift of tangent vectors on (G/H) and then projects back to an (H)-equivariant object. This (\nabla^V) is:

* (G)-invariant,
* metric compatible once (V) carries an (H)-invariant inner product,
* completely determined by the Lie algebra bracket structure fixed earlier.

#### 4.2.2 Exterior covariant derivatives and Laplacians

For each (V), we obtain the covariant exterior derivative
[
d_{\nabla^V}:\Omega^k(S^{15};E_V)\to \Omega^{k+1}(S^{15};E_V),
]
and its adjoint (\delta_{\nabla^V}) with respect to the normal homogeneous metric. The connection Laplacian is
[
\Delta_{\nabla^V} := \nabla^{V\ast}\nabla^V,
]
and the Hodge Laplacian on (E_V)-valued forms is
[
\Delta^V_{\mathrm{Hodge}} := d_{\nabla^V}\delta_{\nabla^V}+\delta_{\nabla^V}d_{\nabla^V}.
]

These operators are canonical, (G)-invariant, and their spectra can be approached representation-theoretically.

### 4.3 Vertical–horizontal operator splitting

This section formalizes the “bundle-KK” decomposition purely geometrically.

Let ({e_a}*{a=1}^7) be a local orthonormal frame for (\mathcal{V}) and ({e*\alpha}*{\alpha=1}^8) a local orthonormal frame for (\mathcal{H}). Then for any (E_V)-valued section (s),
[
\nabla^V s = \sum*{a=1}^7 e^a\otimes \nabla^V_{e_a}s ;+; \sum_{\alpha=1}^8 e^\alpha\otimes \nabla^V_{e_\alpha}s.
]

Define the “vertical” and “horizontal” rough Laplacians by
[
\Delta_{\mathcal{V},V}s := -\sum_{a=1}^7 \left(\nabla^V_{e_a}\nabla^V_{e_a}s - \nabla^V_{\nabla_{e_a}e_a}s\right),
]
[
\Delta_{\mathcal{H},V}s := -\sum_{\alpha=1}^8 \left(\nabla^V_{e_\alpha}\nabla^V_{e_\alpha}s - \nabla^V_{\nabla_{e_\alpha}e_\alpha}s\right),
]
where (\nabla) is the Levi–Civita connection on (S^{15}).

Because (\mathcal{V}) and (\mathcal{H}) are defined by a principal connection, these pieces are globally meaningful (though frames are local).

#### Lemma 4.1 (First-order splitting)

For any (V), the covariant derivative decomposes as
[
\nabla^V = \nabla^V_{\mathcal{V}} ;+; \nabla^V_{\mathcal{H}},
]
where (\nabla^V_{\mathcal{V}}) differentiates only along vertical directions and (\nabla^V_{\mathcal{H}}) only along horizontal directions.

*Proof sketch.* This is immediate from the orthogonal splitting (TS^{15}=\mathcal{V}\oplus\mathcal{H}) and the tensoriality of (\nabla^V) in its vector argument. ∎

---

### 4.4 The first operator decomposition theorem

The key point is that (\Delta_{\nabla^V}) is not, in general, a direct sum of (\Delta_{\mathcal{V},V}) and (\Delta_{\mathcal{H},V}); there is an additional curvature coupling term determined by the connection and the non-integrability of the horizontal distribution.

#### Theorem 4.2 (Canonical Laplacian decomposition)

For any homogeneous associated bundle (E_V\to S^{15}) with its canonical induced connection,
[
\Delta_{\nabla^V} ;=; \Delta_{\mathcal{V},V} ;+; \Delta_{\mathcal{H},V} ;+; \mathcal{R}_V,
]
where (\mathcal{R}_V) is a zeroth-order endomorphism of (E_V) determined canonically by the curvature of the homogeneous connection (equivalently, by the ([\mathfrak{p},\mathfrak{p}]\subset\mathfrak{k}) bracket component fixed in Section 3).

*Proof sketch (structure-level, to be expanded).* The difference between the full rough Laplacian and the sum of vertical and horizontal rough Laplacians is controlled by the O’Neill tensors (or equivalently by curvature of the principal connection) for Riemannian submersions with connection. In the homogeneous case this term is (G)-invariant, hence must be an (H)-equivariant endomorphism of (V), i.e. a zeroth-order bundle endomorphism (\mathcal{R}_V). ∎

**Remark 4.3.** The theorem is purely geometric: it is an operator identity on a fixed homogeneous background. It is the precise sense in which “vertical modes” and “horizontal geometry” couple through curvature.

## 4.5. Dirac Operator Decomposition on the Octonionic Hopf Background

### 4.5.1 Spinor representations and bundle realization

Let (\mathbb{S}\to S^{15}) denote the real spinor bundle of the 15-sphere. Using the homogeneous realization
[
S^{15}\cong G/H=\mathrm{Spin}(9)/\mathrm{Spin}(7),
]
the spinor bundle can be written as an associated bundle
[
\mathbb{S} ;\cong; G\times_H \Sigma_{15},
]
where (\Sigma_{15}) is the real irreducible spin representation of (\mathrm{Spin}(15)), restricted to (H=\mathrm{Spin}(7)) via the isotropy representation.

Under this restriction, (\Sigma_{15}) decomposes into irreducible (\mathrm{Spin}(7))-modules. While the precise decomposition is not required immediately, what matters is that:

* Clifford multiplication by vertical vectors ((\mathfrak{m})) and horizontal vectors ((\mathfrak{p})) act differently on (\Sigma_{15}),
* the Spin(7)-structure on (\mathfrak{p}) introduces additional algebraic constraints on horizontal Clifford multiplication.

---

### 4.5.2 Clifford algebra splitting

Using the orthogonal decomposition
[
TS^{15} = \mathcal{V} \oplus \mathcal{H},
\qquad
\dim \mathcal{V}=7,\quad \dim \mathcal{H}=8,
]
the Clifford algebra splits as
[
\mathrm{Cl}(15) ;\cong; \mathrm{Cl}(\mathcal{V}) ,\widehat{\otimes}, \mathrm{Cl}(\mathcal{H}),
]
where (\widehat{\otimes}) denotes the graded tensor product.

Let ({e_a}*{a=1}^7) be a local orthonormal frame for (\mathcal{V}) and ({e*\alpha}*{\alpha=1}^8) for (\mathcal{H}). Clifford multiplication on spinors satisfies
[
e_a\cdot e*\alpha + e_\alpha\cdot e_a = 0,
]
reflecting orthogonality of vertical and horizontal directions.

---

### 4.5.3 Definition of vertical and horizontal Dirac operators

The Dirac operator on (S^{15}) is
[
D_{S^{15}} = \sum_{i=1}^{15} e_i\cdot \nabla_{e_i},
]
where (\nabla) is the Levi–Civita spin connection.

Using the splitting, define:
[
D_{\mathcal{V}} := \sum_{a=1}^7 e_a\cdot \nabla_{e_a},
\qquad
D_{\mathcal{H}} := \sum_{\alpha=1}^8 e_\alpha\cdot \nabla_{e_\alpha}.
]

Then formally,
[
D_{S^{15}} = D_{\mathcal{V}} + D_{\mathcal{H}}.
]

However, because the horizontal distribution is not integrable, this equality must be corrected by curvature terms.

---

### 4.5.4 Curvature correction and O’Neill-type terms

The Levi–Civita connection decomposes as
[
\nabla = \nabla^{\mathcal{V}} + \nabla^{\mathcal{H}} + \mathcal{A},
]
where (\mathcal{A}) is the O’Neill tensor encoding failure of horizontality under parallel transport. In the homogeneous setting, (\mathcal{A}) is completely determined by the Lie bracket relations
[
[\mathfrak{p},\mathfrak{p}] \subset \mathfrak{k}.
]

Substituting this decomposition into the Dirac operator yields an identity of the form
[
D_{S^{15}} = D_{\mathcal{V}} + D_{\mathcal{H}} + \mathcal{C},
]
where (\mathcal{C}) is a zeroth-order Clifford-algebra–valued endomorphism of (\mathbb{S}) determined canonically by the curvature of the homogeneous connection.

---

### 4.5.5 Spin(7) structure and simplification of curvature terms

Because the curvature of the canonical connection takes values in (\mathfrak{spin}(7)\subset\mathfrak{so}(8)), the correction term (\mathcal{C}) satisfies strong algebraic constraints:

**Proposition 4.4.**
The curvature correction term (\mathcal{C}) commutes with the action of (\mathrm{Spin}(7)) on the horizontal Clifford algebra and annihilates the Spin(7)-invariant subspace of (\Sigma_{15}).

*Interpretation (geometric, not physical).*
This means that the Spin(7)-invariant part of the spinor bundle experiences a cleaner vertical–horizontal separation than generic components. The presence of triality and the Cayley form is not cosmetic; it directly restricts how curvature can couple spinor components.

---

### 4.5.6 Squared Dirac operator and comparison with Laplacians

Squaring the Dirac operator yields the standard Weitzenböck formula:
[
D_{S^{15}}^2 = \nabla^\ast\nabla + \tfrac{1}{4}\mathrm{Scal},
]
where (\mathrm{Scal}) is the scalar curvature of (S^{15}).

Using the decomposition above, one obtains
[
D_{S^{15}}^2
============

D_{\mathcal{V}}^2
+
D_{\mathcal{H}}^2
+
{\text{mixed first-order terms}}
+
{\text{curvature zeroth-order terms}}.
]

In the homogeneous case, the mixed first-order terms cancel after averaging, leaving a decomposition fully controlled by:

* vertical fiber geometry ((S^7)),
* horizontal Spin(7)-geometry ((S^8)),
* canonical curvature couplings fixed in Section 3.

This parallels—but does not assume—any Kaluza–Klein interpretation.
