## Section 2 — Geometric core and the corrected linchpin (Draft v1)

### 2.1. Base geometry: (S^8) and the octonionic projective line

The geometric starting point of NFC is the 8-sphere (S^8), which admits a canonical identification with the octonionic projective line (\mathbb{O}P^1). This identification is not used here to introduce coordinates or local fields, but to fix the global topological and algebraic context: (S^8) is the unique compact, simply connected manifold whose symmetry structure naturally supports octonionic and Spin(9)/Spin(8)-related constructions.

Throughout this paper, (S^8) is equipped with the round metric (g_{S^8}). No deformation of the base geometry is assumed at this stage; all nontrivial structure arises from the associated bundles and their identifications.

### 2.2. Dual associated bundles and precise meaning of “chirality”

Associated to the Spin(8) structure relevant over (S^8) are two inequivalent real 8-dimensional spinor representations, customarily denoted (S^+) and (S^-). In NFC language, these are sometimes referred to as “left” and “right” chiral structures; here we avoid metaphor and state the precise content.

Let
[
\pi_\pm : E_\pm \to S^8
]
be the sphere bundles associated to (S^\pm), with fibers diffeomorphic to (S^7). Each bundle (E_\pm) is a smooth 15-dimensional manifold when considered independently. The terminology “dual associated bundles” refers solely to the fact that both arise from the same Spin(8) principal bundle via inequivalent representations.

At this stage there is **no identification** between (E_+) and (E_-). They are distinct but canonically related through the Spin(8) structure.

### 2.3. Triality and the diagonal identification

Spin(8) admits an outer automorphism group isomorphic to (\mathbb{Z}*3), known as triality, which permutes the vector representation and the two spinor representations. NFC invokes this structure to relate (E*+) and (E_-) nontrivially.

Formally, let (\tau) denote a triality automorphism acting on the representation data. NFC considers a **triality-twisted diagonal identification** of the form
[
(x, v_+, v_-) \sim (x, v_+, \tau(v_+)),
]
or equivalently an identification between fibers of (E_+) and (E_-) mediated by (\tau).

The intent of this identification is to reduce the naïve product structure (E_+ \times_{S^8} E_-) to a single effective total space carrying “both” chiral structures.

### 2.4. Non-freeness of the triality action

A crucial fact—central to this paper—is that the triality-twisted diagonal action is **not free** in general. There exist points in the fibers for which stabilizers are nontrivial. In particular, configurations compatible with octonionic multiplication admit stabilizer subgroups containing (G_2), reflecting the automorphism group of the octonions.

Consequently, the quotient obtained by the triality-twisted identification is **not** a smooth manifold globally. Any claim that the result is smoothly diffeomorphic to (S^{15}) is therefore incorrect without qualification.

This is not a defect to be repaired, but a structural fact to be incorporated.

We therefore define the **triality-stratified total space** (\widetilde{E}) as the quotient of the triality-twisted identification, understood as a stratified space in the standard sense: a space decomposed into smooth strata of varying dimension, glued in a controlled manner.

All differential-geometric constructions in this paper are carried out on (\widetilde{E}_{\mathrm{gen}}). The role of (\Sigma) is purely to impose admissibility and regularity conditions on fields approaching it; (\Sigma) does not carry independent dynamics.

Higher codimension strengthens the results but is not required for the main existence statement.

## Section 4 — Minisuperspace reduction and identification of the dominant instability (Draft v1)

### 4.1. Rationale for a minisuperspace analysis

The linearized operator (\mathcal L) introduced in Section 3 acts on a large space of admissible perturbations on (\widetilde{E}_{\mathrm{gen}}). A complete spectral analysis at this level would require detailed knowledge of the full geometry, bundle structure, and stratification-induced correction terms. Such an analysis is neither necessary nor appropriate for a first-pass viability test.

Instead, we adopt a standard strategy in geometric and gravitational stability analysis: isolate the **most dangerous instability channel** and test stability there first. If instability is unavoidable in that channel, no amount of additional structure elsewhere can rescue the background. Conversely, if stability can be established there on an open parameter region, the framework earns the right to further scrutiny.

In the present setting, this dominant channel is the **relative scaling (breathing) mode** between base and fiber.

---

### 4.2. Connection-metric ansatz on the generic stratum

On the smooth generic stratum (\widetilde{E}*{\mathrm{gen}}), we assume the existence of a fibration-like structure
[
\pi:\widetilde{E}*{\mathrm{gen}}\to S^8
]
together with an Ehresmann connection splitting
[
T\widetilde{E}_{\mathrm{gen}} = H \oplus V,
]
where (H) and (V) denote horizontal and vertical subbundles, respectively.

We restrict attention to background metrics of **connection type**:
[
\bar g ;=; a^2,\pi^* g_{S^8} ;+; b^2,g_F,
]
where:

* (g_{S^8}) is the round metric on the base,
* (g_F) is a fixed compact metric on the fiber (assumed to admit discrete Laplace spectrum and Clifford compatibility),
* (a,b>0) are constant scale parameters.

This ansatz is deliberately minimal. It preserves maximal symmetry compatible with the stratified structure while allowing nontrivial interaction between base and fiber through the connection and curvature terms.

---

### 4.3. Why the breathing mode is the dominant risk

Perturbations of the background metric can be decomposed into:

* trace-free tensor modes on the base,
* trace-free tensor modes on the fiber,
* mixed and vector modes (controlled by gauge fixing),
* and overall scale deformations.

Among these, the most notorious source of instability in product and fibration geometries is the **relative scale deformation**
[
a \longrightarrow a + \delta a,
\qquad
b \longrightarrow b + \delta b,
]
in which one factor expands while the other contracts. This is commonly referred to as the *radion* or *breathing* mode.

Two facts make this mode decisive:

1. It sits at the **lowest harmonic level** (constant on base and fiber), so Laplacian terms do not provide automatic positive contributions.
2. Any runaway behavior here invalidates the background regardless of stability in higher modes.

Therefore, stability of the breathing mode is a **necessary condition** for overall stability.

---

### 4.4. Reduction to scale variables

To analyze this sector cleanly, we restrict the effective functional (S_{\mathrm{eff}}) to the two-dimensional space of scale parameters ((a,b)), holding all other geometric data fixed. This yields a reduced “minisuperspace” functional of the form
[
S_{\mathrm{eff}} ;\longrightarrow; \int dt ; \big( \text{kinetic terms} - V(a,b) \big),
]
where (V(a,b)) is the effective potential governing static configurations.

For stability analysis it is convenient to work in logarithmic variables:
[
x := \ln a,
\qquad
y := \ln b.
]
In these variables, linear stability is governed by the Hessian of
[
W(x,y) := V(e^x,e^y)
]
at a critical point ((x_*,y_*)).

---

### 4.5. Structure of the reduced potential

Each term in the effective functional contributes to (V(a,b)) with a definite scaling determined by:

* the dimension of the base and fiber,
* the tensorial nature of the field (curvature, connection curvature, torsion),
* and the metric weights carried by contractions.

Schematically:

* base curvature terms scale like (a^{6}b^{7}),
* fiber curvature and torsion terms scale like (a^{8}b^{5}),
* vertical curvature energy terms scale like (a^{8}b^{3}).

Thus (V(a,b)) is a finite sum of monomials
[
V(a,b)=\sum_i K_i,a^{p_i}b^{q_i},
]
with explicitly computable exponents ((p_i,q_i)).

This scaling structure, rather than any detailed coefficient values, is what controls the existence and stability of critical points.

---

### 4.6. Criterion for breathing-mode stability

Let ((x_*,y_*)) be a critical point of (W(x,y)). The breathing mode is linearly stable if and only if the log-Hessian
[
\nabla^2 W(x_*,y_*)
]
is positive definite.

Because each monomial contributes a rank-one positive or negative semidefinite matrix proportional to
[
K_i e^{p_i x_* + q_i y_*}
\begin{pmatrix}
p_i \ q_i
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
p_i & q_i
\end{pmatrix},
]
stability is governed by:

1. the presence of **at least two competing scalings** in (b),
2. the existence of a **positive stiffening contribution** with steeper (b)-dependence,
3. dominance of that stiffener in the Hessian at the critical point.

These conditions are made explicit in Sections 5–6.

---

### 4.7. Open-set (non-fine-tuned) stability

A critical requirement for viability is that stability not occur only at a single isolated ratio (a/b). In minisuperspace terms, this means that the Hessian remains positive definite on an **open neighborhood** of ((x_*,y_*)).

Because (V(a,b)) depends smoothly on:

* the scale variables,
* the background connection and torsion data,
* and the geometric invariants entering the coefficients (K_i),

any strict Hessian positivity condition defines an open region in parameter space. This ensures that stability, if present, is not the result of measure-zero tuning.

---

### 4.8. Role of the minisuperspace analysis in the full theory

The minisuperspace reduction does **not** claim to capture all possible instabilities. Its role is diagnostic:

* Failure in the breathing sector is fatal.
* Success in the breathing sector is necessary but not sufficient.

Accordingly, Sections 5–6 establish sufficient conditions for breathing-mode stability, while Sections 7–8 address ellipticity and the remaining triality-even spectral sectors.

---

### 4.9. Summary

Section 4 establishes the logical and technical foundation for the stability analysis:

1. The breathing mode is identified as the dominant instability channel.
2. Its dynamics reduce to a two-variable minisuperspace problem.
3. Stability is determined by the Hessian of a reduced potential with explicitly controlled scaling structure.
4. Non-fine-tuned stability corresponds to Hessian positivity on an open region.

With this reduction in place, the problem of viability becomes concrete and testable, rather than schematic.

V(a,b)

B := \alpha,c_F + \gamma,t_F + \beta,f_{BF},

\qquad
\partial_y W=0

> **Condition (E1):** (C>0) and (B) is not tuned to exactly cancel the stabilizing contribution.

[
\nabla^2 W(x_*,y_*)
===================

\sum_i K_i e^{p_i x_*+q_i y_*}
\begin{pmatrix}
p_i \ q_i
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
p_i & q_i
\end{pmatrix},
]
where the sum runs over monomials (K_i a^{p_i}b^{q_i}).

Stability requires this Hessian to be positive definite. Because each term contributes a rank-one matrix, positivity is controlled by:

1. the signs of the coefficients (K_i),
2. the relative magnitudes of the contributions at the critical point,
3. the linear independence of the exponent vectors ((p_i,q_i)).

## Section 7 — Ellipticity under stratification-compatible boundary filtration (Draft v1)

### 7.1. Why ellipticity is a non-negotiable requirement

The stability analysis of Sections 4–6 presupposes that the linearized equations of motion define a **well-posed spectral problem**. This requires that the linearized operator be elliptic (after gauge fixing) on the domain of admissible perturbations.

In the present setting, ellipticity is not automatic, because the total space (\widetilde{E}) is **stratified rather than smooth**. The purpose of this section is to show that, under explicit and mild hypotheses on the singular locus (\Sigma) and the admissibility conditions imposed near it, ellipticity of the relevant operators on the smooth generic stratum (\widetilde{E}_{\mathrm{gen}}) is preserved.

---

### 7.2. Operators under consideration

Let ((\bar g,\bar A,\bar T)) be a background configuration satisfying the hypotheses of Sections 3–6. After background-covariant gauge fixing and restriction to admissible perturbations, the linearized operator takes the block form
[
\mathcal L =
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathcal O_g & * & * \

* & \mathcal O_A & * \
* & * & \mathcal O_T
  \end{pmatrix},
  ]
  where:

- (\mathcal O_g) is a generalized Lichnerowicz operator acting on metric perturbations,
- (\mathcal O_A) is a connection Laplacian plus curvature terms acting on (\delta A),
- (\mathcal O_T) is an elliptic operator arising from the torsion sector.

Each diagonal block is of **Laplace type**, i.e. its principal symbol is given by (|\xi|^2) times the identity on the relevant bundle.

Ellipticity therefore holds pointwise on (\widetilde{E}_{\mathrm{gen}}). The only potential obstruction arises from the behavior of admissible perturbations near the singular locus (\Sigma).

---

### 7.3. Stratified spaces and admissible function spaces

The singular locus (\Sigma\subset\widetilde{E}) is a closed stratified subset. We assume:

* (\widetilde{E}_{\mathrm{gen}}=\widetilde{E}\setminus\Sigma) is a smooth manifold,
* admissible perturbations are required to be square-integrable ((L^2)) with respect to (\bar g),
* triality filtration suppresses certain representation components but does not impose differential boundary conditions.

Importantly, (\Sigma) is **not** treated as a physical boundary. No Dirichlet, Neumann, or mixed boundary conditions are imposed. Instead, admissibility is encoded through decay and representation-theoretic constraints.

### 7.4. Codimension sufficiency criterion

The key analytic input is the following standard fact from elliptic theory on singular and stratified spaces.

> **Lemma 7.1 (Codimension sufficiency for ellipticity).**
> Let (M) be a smooth manifold and (\Sigma\subset M) a closed subset of codimension (k\ge 3). Let (\mathcal D) be a second-order elliptic operator defined on (M\setminus\Sigma). Then, when acting on (L^2)-admissible sections, (\mathcal D) admits a unique self-adjoint extension and defines a well-posed elliptic spectral problem.

This result is standard in the analysis of conifolds, orbifolds, and manifolds with isolated or higher-codimension singularities. The essential point is that singular sets of codimension (\ge 3) do not support localized (L^2) modes capable of destroying ellipticity or essential self-adjointness.

---

### 7.5. Application to the NFC stratification

In NFC, the singular locus (\Sigma) arises from non-free stabilizers of the triality-twisted identification. For octonion-compatible stabilizers (e.g. containing (G_2)), the expected codimension is high. However, the viability analysis requires only the conservative assumption
[

]

Under this assumption, Lemma 7.1 applies directly to each diagonal block (\mathcal O_g,\mathcal O_A,\mathcal O_T) of the linearized operator.

---

### 7.6. Effect of triality filtration on ellipticity

The triality filtration restricts admissible perturbations by representation type, suppressing triality-odd components near (\Sigma). Crucially:

* the filtration acts **algebraically**, not differentially,
* it does not constrain normal derivatives,
* it does not alter the principal symbol of the operators.

Therefore, ellipticity—being a property of the principal symbol—is unaffected by the triality filtration.

---

### 7.7. Absence of hidden non-elliptic channels

Ellipticity could fail only if one of the following occurred:

1. (\Sigma) had codimension (\le 2),
2. boundary conditions fixed derivatives normal to (\Sigma),
3. representation-theoretic constraints removed entire principal-symbol directions.

None of these occur under the standing hypotheses:

* codimension is assumed (\ge 3),
* admissibility conditions impose decay, not boundary constraints,
* triality filtration preserves full tangent-space access for admissible modes.

> **Result 8.2.**

> **Result 8.3.**

> **Result 8.4.**
> The torsion sector is spectrally non-negative.

# Existence of a Linearly Stable Background in Nested Fibrational Cosmology

## Abstract

We present a viability result for Nested Fibrational Cosmology (NFC) by establishing explicit sufficient conditions for the existence of at least one linearly stable background configuration. The geometric core of NFC is formulated in terms of a triality-controlled octonionic construction over (S^8 \cong \mathbb{O}P^1). We show that the natural triality-twisted identification is generically non-free, necessitating a refinement from a smooth-manifold target to a 15-dimensional triality-stratified total space (\widetilde{E}) with singular locus (\Sigma) and smooth generic stratum (\widetilde{E}*{\mathrm{gen}}=\widetilde{E}\setminus\Sigma). Dynamics and stability are posed on (\widetilde{E}*{\mathrm{gen}}) with stratification-compatible admissibility conditions near (\Sigma). Using a minimal effective functional consisting of curvature, connection-curvature energy, and torsion-like terms, we isolate the dominant instability channel via a minisuperspace reduction to base and fiber scale parameters. We derive explicit sufficient conditions under which this sector admits a stable critical point on an open parameter region. A canonical stabilizing contribution is shown to arise from the vertical curvature energy of a Clifford-compatible, triality-even connection. We further establish ellipticity of the linearized operator under mild codimension assumptions on (\Sigma) and state sufficient spectral non-negativity conditions excluding additional triality-even instabilities. The result is a precise, auditable viability statement for NFC, together with a bounded list of computations required to close the argument in any explicit realization.

## 1. Introduction and statement of goals

Nested Fibrational Cosmology (NFC) is a top-down approach to fundamental structure in which physical degrees of freedom are organized by a hierarchy of fibrations and the algebraic constraints they impose. The motivating premise is that many structures treated as fundamental in effective theories—spacetime geometry, gauge fields, and symmetry groups—should instead be viewed as emergent from a small number of rigid geometric inputs, most notably those associated with the normed-division-algebra ladder and its related Hopf fibrations.

A recurring difficulty in speculative unification programs is the absence of a clear viability threshold. Frameworks are often evaluated on conceptual appeal or breadth rather than on the existence of even a single dynamically meaningful, stable configuration. The purpose of this paper is to supply such a threshold for NFC.

The sole aim of this work is to establish a referee-auditable set of sufficient conditions for the existence of at least one linearly stable background configuration consistent with the corrected geometric core of NFC. The analysis proceeds by formalizing the linchpin geometric identification, correcting a common smoothness assumption, and posing dynamics on the appropriate smooth domain. Stability is addressed first in the most dangerous sector—the relative base–fiber scaling mode—via a controlled minisuperspace reduction, and subsequently through ellipticity and spectral non-negativity conditions in the remaining triality-even sectors.

No attempt is made to derive phenomenology, identify low-energy particle content, or claim uniqueness of the stable background. The scope is intentionally narrow: existence and linear stability under explicit hypotheses.

## 2. Geometric core and the corrected linchpin

The base of the NFC construction is the 8-sphere (S^8), identified with the octonionic projective line (\mathbb{O}P^1). This identification is used only to fix global topological and algebraic context. The base is equipped throughout with the round metric (g_{S^8}).

Associated to the relevant Spin(8) structure over (S^8) are two inequivalent real eight-dimensional spinor representations. Let
[
\pi_\pm : E_\pm \to S^8
]
denote the corresponding associated sphere bundles, each with fiber diffeomorphic to (S^7). These bundles are smooth manifolds when considered independently. The terminology “dual chiral bundles” refers solely to their origin from inequivalent Spin(8) representations.

Spin(8) admits an outer automorphism group isomorphic to (\mathbb{Z}*3), known as triality, which permutes the vector and spinor representations. NFC invokes this structure to relate (E*+) and (E_-) via a triality-twisted diagonal identification. Formally, the identification relates points in the two bundles through the action of a triality automorphism on the representation data.

A crucial structural fact is that this triality-twisted identification is generically non-free. There exist points with nontrivial stabilizers, including stabilizers containing (G_2), reflecting the automorphism group of the octonions. Consequently, the quotient obtained from this identification is not a smooth manifold globally.

The appropriate geometric object is therefore a 15-dimensional stratified space (\widetilde{E}). Its singular locus (\Sigma) consists of points with nontrivial stabilizer under the triality action. The complement
[
\widetilde{E}_{\mathrm{gen}}=\widetilde{E}\setminus\Sigma
]
is a smooth 15-dimensional manifold, referred to as the generic stratum.

All differential-geometric constructions in this paper are carried out on (\widetilde{E}_{\mathrm{gen}}). The singular locus (\Sigma) plays no dynamical role; it enters only through admissibility and regularity conditions on fields approaching it. We assume throughout that (\mathrm{codim}(\Sigma)\ge 3), which suffices for the analytic results used later.

## 4. Minisuperspace reduction and the dominant instability

A full spectral analysis of the linearized operator would require detailed information about the entire geometry. For a viability test, it is sufficient to isolate the dominant instability channel. In fibration geometries this channel is the relative scaling, or breathing, mode between base and fiber.

The breathing mode corresponds to uniform variations of (a) and (b). Because it is constant on base and fiber, it receives no automatic positive contribution from Laplacians and is therefore the most dangerous potential instability.

Restricting the effective functional to the space of scale parameters yields a reduced potential (V(a,b)). Introducing logarithmic variables (x=\ln a), (y=\ln b), linear stability is governed by the Hessian of (W(x,y)=V(e^x,e^y)) at a critical point.

## 5. Reduced potential and sufficient conditions for stability
