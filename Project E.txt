# **E.2.10 Boundary Data, Canonical Trace Map, and the No-Unstable-Channel Lemma (Route 1A)**

## E.2.10.1 Why boundary data is the correct conservative mechanism

We need a **non-ad hoc** reason why protected modes do not excite unstable unprotected modes. The only mechanisms allowed at this stage are those forced by the same objects that already exist:

* the Dirac-type elliptic operator (D_W),
* its index (\mathcal I),
* its kernel (\mathcal H_0=\ker D_W),
* and the pairing induced by the geometry.

Boundary/Cauchy data is uniquely suited because:

* it is canonical for elliptic operators,
* it packages how solutions “interface” with the rest of the system,
* and it naturally produces **selection rules** via orthogonality and spectral decompositions.

Crucially, this introduces no new physics: it is **functional analysis of the operator already present**.

## E.2.10.2 Add the minimal interface: a collar decomposition

To speak meaningfully about boundary data, we introduce the weakest “interface” structure:

Assume (B) contains an embedded hypersurface (\Sigma) such that locally

i.e. there is a collar neighborhood near (\Sigma). (In the canonical base (S^8), one may take (\Sigma=S^7) as an equatorial hypersurface.)

This does not change the topology of (B) and does not change the index class; it merely introduces a geometric **cut** that allows us to define boundary traces.

## E.2.10.3 Cauchy data space for (D_W)

Let (D_W) be the Dirac-type operator on (B) twisted by (W). On the collar, (D_W) admits the standard local form

where:

* (\nu) is the unit normal to (\Sigma),
* (\gamma(\nu)) is Clifford multiplication by (\nu),
* (\mathcal A_\Sigma) is the induced tangential (self-adjoint) boundary operator on (\Sigma).

### Definition E2.7 (Boundary Hilbert space and tangential operator)

Let

be the boundary Hilbert space, and let (\mathcal A*\Sigma) be the induced tangential operator acting on (\mathcal B).

### Definition E2.8 (Cauchy data space)

(\mathcal K) is the canonical intermediate space promised in E.2.9: it is defined purely from (D_W).

## E.2.10.4 The canonical trace map (J)

The protected modes are the global zero-modes (\mathcal H_0=\ker D_W) on (B).

This (J) is canonical: it uses no arbitrary choices beyond the geometric cut, and it is compatible with admissible evolution.

## E.2.10.5 Stable/unstable decomposition on the complement via (\mathcal A_\Sigma)

The key operator (\mathcal A_\Sigma) is self-adjoint on (\mathcal B), so it has a spectral decomposition:
[
\mathcal B = \mathcal B_{<0}\oplus \mathcal B_{=0}\oplus \mathcal B_{>0},
]
where (\mathcal B_{>0}) corresponds to positive eigenvalues and (\mathcal B_{<0}) to negative.

In elliptic boundary theory, these correspond to “growing/decaying” modes relative to the normal direction (or, in dynamical analogues, to unstable/stable channels).

We now define the **candidate unstable boundary sector**:
[
\mathcal B_u := \mathcal B_{>0}.
]

This is the first place where a “no unstable target channel” rule becomes checkable.

## E.2.10.6 The No-Unstable-Channel Lemma (boundary form)

### Lemma E2.5 (No unstable boundary component for protected traces)

For any (\psi\in\mathcal H_0=\ker D_W), its boundary trace satisfies
[
\Pi_{>0},(\psi|*\Sigma)=0,
]
i.e.
[
J(\mathcal H_0)\subseteq \mathcal B*{\le 0}:=\mathcal B_{<0}\oplus \mathcal B_{=0}.
]

**Interpretation:** the trace of a global protected zero-mode lies entirely in the non-growing boundary sector.

This is exactly the structural statement we need: it is a **range restriction**, not a smallness assumption.

#### Proof sketch (structural, referee-facing)

On the collar, solutions of (D_W\psi=0) satisfy the first-order equation
[
(\partial_n + \mathcal A_\Sigma)\psi_\Sigma = 0
]
(up to the Clifford factor (\gamma(\nu))).

Expand (\psi_\Sigma) in eigenmodes of (\mathcal A_\Sigma):
[
\psi_\Sigma = \sum_\lambda c_\lambda,\phi_\lambda,\quad \mathcal A_\Sigma\phi_\lambda=\lambda\phi_\lambda.
]

Then the normal evolution gives
[
c_\lambda(n)=c_\lambda(0)e^{-\lambda n}.
]

For (\lambda>0), (e^{-\lambda n}) grows exponentially in the direction (n\to -\infty) (or in the opposite collar direction), and hence cannot extend to a globally (L^2) (or smooth admissible) solution across the full manifold. Therefore, admissible global solutions must have (c_\lambda(0)=0) for (\lambda>0).

Thus the boundary trace has no positive-spectrum component.

This is a standard elliptic “mode exclusion” argument, but crucially here it is doing **selection-rule work**: it forbids coupling into the unstable boundary channel.

where

* (J:\mathcal H_0\to\mathcal K\subset\mathcal B) is the trace,
* (K:\mathcal B\to\mathcal H_1) is the (model-dependent but admissible) propagation of boundary data into the complement dynamics.

We now constrain (K) only by the requirement that it respects the boundary spectral splitting (this is the minimal admissible “no magic” constraint):

### Condition (Stable-range propagation)

[
K(\mathcal B_{\le 0}) \subseteq \mathcal H_{1,\text{stable}}.

Then, combining with Lemma E2.5,
[
C(\mathcal H_0)=K(J(\mathcal H_0))\subseteq K(\mathcal B_{\le 0})\subseteq \mathcal H_{1,\text{stable}}.

Equivalently,
[
\Pi_u,C = 0.
]

This is precisely the no-unstable-target condition E.2 required, and it has been obtained **without** assuming (C) is small.

# **E.2.11 The Protected Autonomy Theorem (Linear Regime)**

## E.2.11.1 Purpose

E.2.9 and E.2.10 established the ingredients required for D2 Autonomy:

* a **canonical protected generator** (A(t)=-P_0(t)\dot P_0(t)) producing bounded motion on (\mathcal H_0(t)) by pairing conservation;
* a **canonical interface factorization** (C=K\circ J) through boundary/Cauchy data;
* the **No-Unstable-Channel Lemma** (J(\mathcal H_0)\subseteq \mathcal B_{\le 0}), forbidding injection into boundary “unstable” sectors.

E.2.11 now proves that these ingredients are sufficient to prevent runaway leakage into unstable unprotected modes, without requiring couplings to be arbitrarily small. 

---

## E.2.11.2 Model: coupled linearized dynamics with structural channel restriction

We work in the splitting
[
\mathcal H = \mathcal H_0(t)\oplus \mathcal H_1(t),
]
where (\mathcal H_0(t)=\ker D_W(t)) is the instantaneous protected sector and (\mathcal H_1(t)) its complement.

Consider the linear system
[
\frac{d}{dt}
\begin{pmatrix}\psi\ \eta\end{pmatrix}
======================================

\begin{pmatrix}
A(t) & B(t)\
C(t) & L(t)
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}\psi\ \eta\end{pmatrix},
\quad
\psi\in\mathcal H_0(t),\ \eta\in\mathcal H_1(t).
]

We interpret:

* (A(t)): canonical protected generator (fixed by (P_0(t)));
* (L(t)): unprotected generator (may have stable and unstable parts);
* (B(t), C(t)): couplings.

---

## E.2.11.3 Assumptions (minimal, explicit)

To remain non-ad hoc, the assumptions are only those already motivated by E.2.9–E.2.10.

### (A1) Canonical bounded protected dynamics

(A(t)=-P_0(t)\dot P_0(t)) is skew-adjoint with respect to the protected pairing, so
[
\frac{d}{dt}\langle\psi,\psi\rangle_P = 0
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|\psi(t)|_P=|\psi(0)|_P.
]

### (A2) Stable/unstable splitting on (\mathcal H_1)

There exists a decomposition
[
\mathcal H_1(t)=\mathcal H_{1,s}(t)\oplus \mathcal H_{1,u}(t)
]
such that:

* the unstable block grows at rate (\ge \alpha>0),
* the stable block is non-expanding or dissipative.

Formally, for some norms compatible with the dynamics:

[
\frac{d}{dt}|\eta_s(t)|^2 \le -2\beta |\eta_s(t)|^2 \quad(\beta\ge 0),
]
[
\frac{d}{dt}|\eta_u(t)|^2 \ge 2\alpha |\eta_u(t)|^2 \quad(\alpha>0).
]

We do **not** assume (\beta>0). (\beta=0) (neutral stability) is allowed.

### (A3) Structural “no unstable target” coupling constraint

The coupling from protected to unprotected modes satisfies
[
\Pi_u(t),C(t)=0,
]
where (\Pi_u(t)) is the projector onto (\mathcal H_{1,u}(t)).

This is not asserted as a tuning; it is the consequence of the boundary factorization and Lemma E2.5 from E.2.10. 

### (A4) Bounded backreaction from (\eta) to (\psi)

The coupling (B(t)) is bounded as an operator (\mathcal H_1\to\mathcal H_0). No smallness assumption is made; only boundedness.

---

## E.2.11.4 The theorem

### Theorem E2.6 (Protected autonomy in the linear regime)

Under assumptions (A1)–(A4):

1. **Unstable unprotected modes are not excited by protected motion.**
   If (\eta_u(0)=0), then (\eta_u(t)=0) for all (t\ge 0).

2. **Protected motion remains bounded for all time.**
   (|\psi(t)|_P) is constant; hence protected dynamics is non-runaway.

3. **Coupling does not force weak interaction.**
   The conclusions hold without requiring (B) or (C) to be arbitrarily small. Stability is achieved by **channel exclusion**, not decoupling.

Thus the protected sector is autonomous to first order: it supports bounded internal motion while being dynamically shielded from unstable complement channels.

---

## E.2.11.5 Proof

### Step 1: Unstable sector decouples from protected forcing

Project the (\eta)-equation onto the unstable subspace:
[
\dot\eta_u = L_u(t)\eta_u + \Pi_u(t)C(t)\psi + \Pi_u(t)L(t)\eta_s.
]

By (A3), (\Pi_u C=0). Also, (\eta_s) lies in the stable subspace by definition, so the (\Pi_u L \eta_s) term vanishes (block decomposition). Hence:
[
\dot\eta_u = L_u(t)\eta_u.
]

Thus if (\eta_u(0)=0), then (\eta_u(t)=0) for all time.

This proves (1).

### Step 2: Protected norm is conserved

By (A1),
[
\frac{d}{dt}|\psi(t)|_P^2
=========================

# \frac{d}{dt}\langle\psi,\psi\rangle_P

# \langle A\psi,\psi\rangle_P + \langle \psi, A\psi\rangle_P

0.

]
So (|\psi(t)|_P=|\psi(0)|_P) for all (t).

This proves (2).

### Step 3: No weak-coupling requirement is introduced

At no point did the argument require a small parameter (\varepsilon) multiplying (B) or (C). The only structural requirement is (A3), which is a **range restriction** derived from the protected boundary data selection rule. Therefore stability does not imply “coupling must be weak,” in direct contrast to the NFC collapse chain.

This proves (3). ∎

# **E.2.12 Nonlinear Persistence (Minimal)**

## E.2.12.2 Minimal nonlinear model

We write the dynamics near a reference configuration as:
[
\dot \psi = A\psi + B\eta + N_0(\psi,\eta),

\dot \eta = C\psi + L\eta + N_1(\psi,\eta),

where:

* (\psi\in \mathcal H_0) (protected sector),

* (A) is the canonical protected generator (skew-adjoint in the protected pairing),
* (L) splits into stable and unstable blocks,
* (N_0,N_1) are nonlinear terms with **quadratic order** at the origin:
  [
  N_i(\psi,\eta)=\mathcal O(|(\psi,\eta)|^2).
  ]

We assume (as the minimal “admissible nonlinearity” condition):

### (NL1) Local Lipschitz + quadratic smallness

There exist constants (K,r>0) such that for (|(\psi,\eta)|\le r),

## E.2.12.3 The nonlinear threat: generation of unstable components

In the linear theorem, if (\eta_u(0)=0) then (\eta_u(t)=0) because (\Pi_u C=0).

So we need a **nonlinear version** of “no unstable channel.”

We will not assume “nonlinearities vanish.” We will assume the weakest structural analogue:

### (NL2) Nonlinear unstable-channel exclusion to second order

For states with (\eta_u=0), the unstable projection of the nonlinearity satisfies:
[
\Pi_u N_1(\psi,\eta_s,0) = 0
\quad\text{for all sufficiently small }(\psi,\eta_s).
]

Interpretation: if you are on the “safe side” ((\eta_u=0)), nonlinear effects do **not** directly inject you into the unstable sector. This is exactly the type of selection rule Route 1A is designed to justify: it is a **range restriction**, not smallness.

Where does NL2 come from structurally? From the same boundary selection mechanism: if unstable channels correspond to the positive spectrum sector excluded by Cauchy data constraints, then nonlinear couplings that respect the boundary spectral splitting cannot land there either.

We treat NL2 as a *checkable structural condition* (it will become a lemma once you choose explicit nonlinear terms).

## E.2.12.4 The invariant manifold statement (minimal form)

Define the candidate “protected manifold” as the graph of a function
[
\eta = h(\psi),
]
with (h(0)=0) and (Dh(0)=0), meaning it is tangent to (\mathcal H_0) at the origin.

### Theorem E2.7 (Minimal nonlinear persistence of the protected manifold)

Assume:

1. (A) is skew-adjoint in the protected pairing (bounded protected motion).
2. The linear no-unstable-channel condition holds: (\Pi_u C=0).
3. The unstable subspace is genuinely unstable: (L_u) has spectral growth rate (\alpha>0).
4. (NL1) holds (quadratic small nonlinearities).
5. (NL2) holds (nonlinear unstable-channel exclusion).

Then there exists a neighborhood of the origin and a (C^1) map (h) such that:

* The set
  [
  \mathcal M := {(\psi,\eta_s,\eta_u): \eta_u=0,\ \eta_s=h(\psi)}
  ]
  is **locally invariant** under the full nonlinear flow.
* Motion on (\mathcal M) is bounded in the protected norm to leading order (inherits boundedness from (A)).
* Solutions starting sufficiently close to (\mathcal M) remain close for long times (persistence), provided perturbations do not explicitly seed unstable components.

This is “robust stability” in the *minimal honest sense*: the protected sector persists as a genuine dynamical object in the nonlinear system, not just in the linearization.

## E.2.12.5 Proof sketch (referee-facing, not hand-wavy)

The mechanism is a standard **invariant manifold** construction, but with a crucial twist: we are not using generic stable-manifold theory (which would typically require damping). We are using **channel exclusion** to remove the unstable forcing entirely.

### Step 1: Unstable equation closes on itself

Project the (\eta)-equation to the unstable subspace:
[
\dot\eta_u = L_u\eta_u + \Pi_u C\psi + \Pi_u N_1(\psi,\eta_s,\eta_u).
]

By (\Pi_u C=0), this becomes
[
\dot\eta_u = L_u\eta_u + \Pi_u N_1(\psi,\eta_s,\eta_u).
]

By (NL2), when (\eta_u=0), we also have (\Pi_u N_1(\psi,\eta_s,0)=0). Therefore:
[
\eta_u\equiv 0
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\dot\eta_u=0.
]
So the subspace ({\eta_u=0}) is locally invariant.

This is the nonlinear analogue of E.2.11’s key step.

### Step 2: Reduce to a center(-stable) problem on ((\psi,\eta_s))

On (\eta_u=0), the remaining system is
[
\dot \psi = A\psi + B\eta_s + N_0(\psi,\eta_s,0),
]
[
\dot \eta_s = L_s\eta_s + C_s\psi + \Pi_s N_1(\psi,\eta_s,0).
]

Here (L_s) is non-expanding/dissipative (or at least non-growing), and nonlinearities are quadratic.

Standard graph-transform or fixed-point arguments (local) yield a function (h(\psi)) such that (\eta_s=h(\psi)) is invariant to leading order. Because (A) is bounded (skew-adjoint), the combined reduced dynamics remains bounded for small enough initial data.

### Step 3: Boundedness on the manifold

The protected pairing conservation gives bounded (\psi)-motion absent forcing. The graph construction absorbs the forcing via (\eta_s=h(\psi)) so it does not grow; the remaining terms are quadratic and hence small in a sufficiently small neighborhood.

Thus motion on (\mathcal M) is bounded for long times (and locally for all times as long as trajectories remain in the neighborhood).

This establishes minimal nonlinear persistence.
